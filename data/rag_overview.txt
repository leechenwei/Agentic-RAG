Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

RAG is a technique that combines a retrieval system with a generative large
language model. Instead of relying solely on parameters learned during training,
the model is given relevant passages fetched from an external knowledge base at
query time, and conditioned on them to produce its answer. This grounds the
model in factual, up-to-date, or domain-specific information and reduces
hallucination.

A typical RAG pipeline has three stages: indexing, retrieval, and generation.
During indexing, documents are split into chunks and converted into vector
embeddings stored in a vector database. At query time, the user's question is
embedded with the same model and used to find the top-k most semantically
similar chunks. Those chunks are then concatenated into the prompt and sent to
the LLM, which produces the final answer.

There are several variants. Naive RAG performs a single retrieval per query.
Advanced RAG adds query rewriting, hybrid search combining keyword and vector
matching, and re-ranking. Agentic RAG exposes retrieval as a tool that the LLM
itself decides when and how to call, enabling multi-step reasoning, query
decomposition, and self-correction. Graph RAG builds a knowledge graph from
the corpus during indexing and traverses entity relationships during retrieval,
which excels at multi-hop questions over highly structured domains.

Quality is measured with metrics such as recall@k and precision@k for the
retrieval stage, and faithfulness, answer relevance, and citation accuracy for
the generation stage. Frameworks like RAGAS and TruLens automate these
evaluations.

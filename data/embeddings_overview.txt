Text Embeddings

A text embedding is a fixed-length vector representation of a piece of text
produced by a neural network. Texts with similar meaning produce vectors that
are close together in the embedding space, which enables semantic search,
clustering, classification, and recommendation without relying on exact keyword
overlap.

Modern embedding models are derived from transformer architectures. Bi-encoder
models such as the Sentence-Transformers family (for example all-MiniLM-L6-v2,
which produces 384-dimensional vectors, or bge-base-en, which produces 768
dimensions) encode each piece of text independently. This makes them fast and
cacheable, which is why they are used for indexing and first-stage retrieval.
Cross-encoder models such as bge-reranker take a query and a candidate
document together and produce a single relevance score; they are far more
accurate but cannot be precomputed, so they are typically applied only to the
top 20 to 100 candidates returned by a bi-encoder.

Embedding quality is evaluated with benchmarks such as MTEB (Massive Text
Embedding Benchmark), which scores models across retrieval, classification,
clustering, and other tasks. Choice of model trades off dimensionality (memory
and compute), inference speed, and accuracy. For most general-purpose RAG use
cases all-MiniLM-L6-v2 is a strong default; for higher accuracy at the cost
of larger vectors, bge-large-en or text-embedding-3-large from OpenAI are
common upgrades.

It is critical to use the SAME embedding model at indexing time and at query
time. Embeddings from different models live in incompatible spaces and cannot
be compared meaningfully.
